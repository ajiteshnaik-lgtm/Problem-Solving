01 — INTRODUCTION
WHAT IS
PROBLEM SOLVING?

Problem-solving is the process of moving from a current state (the problem) to a desired state (the solution) — using logic, creativity, and prior knowledge.

It's not just a school skill. Employers consistently rank it as a top-three skill above technical knowledge. The World Economic Forum lists it as a core 21st-century capability. You use it constantly — every single day.

So why do people fail? Not because of low intelligence — but because of thinking patterns. Our brains take shortcuts, over-rely on past experience, and fail to see problems from fresh angles. These cognitive barriers are exactly what we're here to dismantle.

Definition

The Cognitive Process

Identifying an obstacle, analysing it, and systematically working toward an effective resolution — using both rational and creative thinking.

The Core Problem

Mental Blocks

We impose invisible constraints on ourselves. We default to old strategies. We see what we expect to see. Understanding these patterns is the first step to overcoming them.

Key Insight

It's a Skill — Not a Talent

Problem-solving can be learned, practised, and improved. The strategies and frameworks on this site give you a toolkit you can apply to almost any situation.

02 — TYPES OF PROBLEMS
NOT ALL
PROBLEMS ARE EQUAL

Before you can solve a problem, you need to know what kind of problem you're dealing with. The strategy changes depending on the type.

Well-Defined

Clear goal, known rules, obvious solution. You know when you've solved it.

eg. What is 847 ÷ 7?

VS

Ill-Defined

Vague goal, incomplete information, multiple valid solutions possible.

eg. How do we reduce homelessness?

🟢

SIMPLE

Clear solution, established procedure. Low complexity, low stakes.

eg. Fixing a flat tyre
🟠

COMPLEX

Many variables, significant analysis needed — but solutions exist.

eg. Designing a bridge
🟣

WICKED

No single correct answer. Solving one part creates new problems. Requires ongoing management.

eg. Climate change, poverty
coined by horst rittel — urban planner

What makes a problem "wicked"?

Wicked problems are not just difficult — they're fundamentally resistant to resolution. Every attempt to solve them changes the problem itself. Climate change, poverty, and public health crises all fall into this category. They can't be "solved" once and for all — only managed over time.

03 — FRAMEWORKS
HOW TO
APPROACH IT

Frameworks give you a structured path through a problem. They don't tell you what to think — they tell you how to think.

Developed by Bransford & Stein (1984). One of the most widely taught structured approaches to problem-solving.

I
Identify
Recognise a problem exists. Define what it actually is — not just the symptoms.
D
Define
What needs to change? What are the constraints? What counts as a solution?
E
Explore
Brainstorm multiple strategies before committing to one.
A
Act
Implement your chosen strategy.
L
Look Back
Did it work? What would you do differently?
⚡ The Look Back step is the most commonly skipped — but it's what separates people who improve from those who repeat the same mistakes.

Developed at Stanford's d.school. Used by Apple, IDEO, and Google. Best for ill-defined and wicked problems because it starts with people, not assumptions.

04 — STRATEGIES
YOUR
TOOLKIT

Different problems need different tools. Click each strategy to expand it.

01 Trial and Error ▼

Trying possible solutions one at a time until one works — no systematic plan required.

eg. Trying passwords until you get it right Best when: few options, low stakes
02 Algorithms ▼

A guaranteed step-by-step procedure. If you follow it correctly, you will reach the right answer — every time.

eg. Long division, solving a quadratic, a recipe Best when: certainty matters more than speed
03 Heuristics ▼

Mental shortcuts — rules of thumb — that reach a good-enough answer quickly. No guarantee of being correct, but highly efficient. Most everyday thinking is heuristic.

eg. Chess: "control the centre" rather than calculating every move Best when: speed matters, exact answer not needed
04 Means-End Analysis ▼

Compare your current state to your goal, then work to reduce the gap step by step. Each sub-goal brings you closer to the endpoint.

eg. 1500-word essay → intro → 3 paragraphs → conclusion Best when: goal is clear, path is not
05 Working Backwards ▼

Start from the desired goal and reverse-engineer the steps needed to get there. Common in project planning, maths proofs, and detective reasoning.

eg. 9am train → leave at 8:30 → wake at 7:45 Best when: end goal is clear, starting point is not
05 — WHEN STRATEGIES FAIL
THE TRAPS
IN YOUR BRAIN

Even with the right toolkit, our own minds can sabotage us. Here's what to watch for.

Functional Fixedness

You only see an object as its intended purpose — blocking creative solutions. Duncker's candle problem (1945): given a candle, thumbtacks, and matches, most people failed to tack the box to the wall and use it as a shelf — because they saw the box as a container, not a platform.

Fix: ignore the label. What else could this be?

Mental Set / Fixation

Defaulting to strategies that worked before — even when they're wrong for the new situation. Chess players often miss the best move because a familiar (but inferior) move comes to mind first. The Einstellung Effect.

Fix: flexibility over habit. Try the unfamiliar path first.

Cognitive Overload

When a problem exceeds working memory's capacity — roughly 7 items at once (Miller's Law) — performance collapses. Errors increase, information gets missed, people give up.

Fix: break it down. Write it out. Use IDEAL.

Cognitive Biases

Confirmation bias — only seeking evidence that supports your existing solution.

Anchoring — the first piece of information becomes disproportionately important.

Sunk-cost fallacy — continuing a failing approach because you've already invested in it.

Fix: actively seek disconfirming evidence.
06 — REAL WORLD
THESE STRATEGIES
IN ACTION

Problem-solving isn't theoretical. These are real cases where the right — or wrong — approach changed everything.

Medicine

John Snow & the 1854 Cholera Outbreak

When cholera swept Soho, the accepted theory was "bad air." Snow rejected it, mapped every death, and traced the source to a single contaminated water pump on Broad Street. He had the pump handle removed — and the outbreak ended.

Breaking through mental set with evidence
Engineering

NASA Apollo 13

An oxygen tank exploded. The CO₂ filters were incompatible between modules — square peg, round hole. Engineers used only what was on the spacecraft — cardboard, bags, tape, a sock — to improvise a working adaptor in under two hours.

Overcoming functional fixedness under pressure
Design / Everyday

IDEO's Shopping Cart Redesign

Given 5 days to redesign a supermarket trolley, IDEO didn't start with the cart — they interviewed parents, shoppers, and staff. The result: a radically better design. Starting with people rather than assumptions is the heart of Design Thinking.

Design Thinking in practice
History

The Wright Brothers

For centuries, people tried to fly by mimicking birds. All failed. The Wright Brothers reframed the question — not "how do birds fly?" but "what does a flying machine actually need to do?" That reframe led to the first successful flight in 1903.

Problem definition changes everything
